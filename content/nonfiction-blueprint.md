# Nonfiction Blueprint: Full 21-Day Methodology

## Overview

You can use whatever writing tool that you want. We will focus on using the phrase "writing space" as much as possible.

You can can use pen and paper, pencil and paper, a text editor, a word processor, dictate, whatever you want. These are TOOLS.

Most people are too busy mastering tools and use that as an excuse or a reason not to write their book.

Don't let the excuse about the TOOL become the reason you don't create your book. Use whatever TOOL you feel most comfortable with.


We're going to show you how to get a book out of your brain and into the real world.

The methodology breaks into 4 phases across 21 days:

- **Phase 1 (Day 1):** Foundation - brain dump, organize, title, purpose, audience, outline
- **Phase 2 (Days 2-13):** Writing - elaborate topics, write all chapters
- **Phase 3 (Day 14):** Research - fact-checking and filling gaps
- **Phase 4 (Days 15-21):** Editing & Polish - first draft, read-through, clean up, finalize

### Daily Ritual (Every Day)
Before starting work each day, set a timer for one minute and say aloud:
> "I am now a writer. I am an author. I am focused on the tasks I have to do today, and I eliminate all excuses and distractions."

---

## Pre-Work: Define Your Target Audience

Before you write a single word, get crystal clear on who you're writing for. Go beyond vague demographics.
Picture a real person:
their age,
where they live,
their education level,
whether they're married,
how big their household is,
what job they have,
what interests they have.
What problems keep them up at night that your book solves?
What language (jargon) do they actually use when they talk about this stuff?
This matters for two reasons. First, it keeps your writing focused. When you know exactly who's reading, you stop wandering off into topics that don't serve them. Second, it makes your book sellable.
Publishers don't care about great writing, they care about clear markets.
If you can say "this book is for X people and there are Y million of them," you've got their attention.

If you're going to self-publish, the clarity will keep you focused and make your book attractive to your market.
Know your reader. Know your market. Everything else flows from there.

## Day 1: Foundation (Brain Dump First)

### Steps (timed exercises):

**Step 1: Brain dump (10-minute timer) — DO THIS FIRST**
- Get paper and pen.
- As quickly as possible, from memory, list everything a person needs to know about the subject of your book
- Do NOT write sentences. List single words or phrases only
- Do NOT worry about the order
- Just write

**Step 2: Organize your list**
- When the timer finishes, number your items in a logical order
- Congratulations: you have just created your table of contents, and each topic is a chapter title

**Step 3: Quick captures (no timer)**
- Write down the title you chose (don't get hung up on this or use this as an excuse to procrastinate. This is just a working title.)
- Write the purpose of your book in one sentence
- Write a one to two paragraph description of your target audience (who needs this book, be specific)

**Step 4: Build chapter outlines**
- Count how many topics (chapter titles) are on your list
- Get the exact same number of blank sheets of paper
- Write one topic as a title at the top of each blank sheet
- Set timer for 10 minutes again
- Take any one titled sheet and list everything a person needs to know about that particular topic (single words/phrases, no sentences, no particular order)
- When finished, number the items in the order they should go
- You have just outlined a chapter with ordered subject headings (subtopics)
- Repeat this process for each titled sheet until you have a complete outline of each chapter and your entire book

The whole outlining process often takes about 30 minutes. Even if it takes an hour, you've outlined your whole book in one day.

## When you go through the list and start building chapter outlines, you will probably find that many of the things that you had on your initial list as chapters are actually sub-chapters. These belong nested as sub-chapters inside of your other chapters. For instance, if your initial list includes 'Marketing Strategies' and 'Social Media,' you might realize that 'Social Media' is actually a sub-chapter within 'Marketing Strategies.'

## Day 2: Elaborate On Your Topics

### Steps:

1. Transcribe any notes from spiral notebook/recorder into your writing space
2. Say focus statements (1-minute timer)

**Then:**
- List 8 to 12 reasons why your audience would need your book. What benefits can they gain from reading it? What can they learn and why should they learn it?
- Write a summary of what your book will be about and why, using the reasons above. This will later become the introduction to your book.
- Select any chapter that interests you most at the time and write an introductory paragraph for that chapter
- Then fill in each subject heading (subtopic) by explaining it with complete sentences organized in paragraphs, 1 to 10 paragraphs per subtopic

---

## Days 3-13: Chapters (The Writing Phase)

### Daily routine for each day:

1. Transcribe notes from spiral notebook into appropriate chapters/topic sheets
2. Set timer for 1 minute, say focus statements

**Then:**
- Put your temporary book title at the top of each page in ALL CAPS BOLD
- Put each working chapter title in Upper and Lower Case Bold
- List all subtopics under it in the order you numbered them
- Double space. It's getting more official.
- Then repeat the steps from Day 2: start with the topic that interests you most at the moment, write sentences and paragraphs that sufficiently explain each subtopic
- Repeat for all subtopics in the chapter
- Repeat for all chapters

### Key principles for Days 3-13:
- Writing chapters becomes "a game of fill in the blanks" because you already have a complete outline
- Write an introductory paragraph for each chapter, then go to the first subject heading and explain it in paragraphs
- Do it again for all subjects. When finished with every subtopic, you've written a chapter. When finished with each chapter, you have your completed book.
- Write a conclusion and you're finished
- Pace yourself: do no more and no less than a certain number of chapters each day so that by Day 13 you've done them all
- Write the way you talk. Avoid sounding bookish. Use simple, clear, and direct language.
- **Don't try to polish your writing as you go.** Edit later. Don't let anything stop you.
- Put a question mark (?) next to any subtopic you're not sure you want to include
- Put an asterisk (*) next to any subtopic you need more research or information about
- But keep writing.

---

## Day 14: Research And Fact Checking

1. Say focus statements (1-minute timer)
2. Do your research.
3. Take a list of every subtopic you marked with a star (*) or question mark (?)
4. Thoroughly research all of the information you marked and need. This is your only day for research, so use time wisely.
5. When you return, incorporate new information into your subtopics if useful, or delete the subtopic if it no longer seems useful.

### Important copyright note:
- Do not violate copyrights of others and do not paraphrase
- Make sure you understand the information and explain it in your own words, in your own way
- If you use information from someone, credit the proper source

---

## Day 15: The First Draft

1. Transcribe notes into appropriate chapters
2. Set timer, say focus statements

### Tasks:
- Continue to make corrections from your research into your manuscript
- Organize your chapters: get out your handwritten Topic Sheets and decide if the order needs to change
- Move topics accordingly and write an updated Table of Contents, placed before the first chapter
- Write a summary of what you have told your reader and why, making sure you point out how the information has benefited the reader. Place this after the last chapter as a conclusion.
- Print today's version of your manuscript on light pink paper (so you'll recognize it later)
- Separate your chapters with paper clips or folders

---

## Day 16: The Read-Through

1. Transcribe notes, set timer, say focus statements

### Tasks:
- Get out the pink version of your manuscript. Read it straight through.
- Note any corrections as you go, and write them directly on the pink pages
- Make notes in the margins for any new ideas or changes you'd like to make
- At the end of each chapter, stop reading and ask yourself: "Have I explained this thoroughly? Clearly? Simply? What was the purpose of this chapter? Have I fulfilled that purpose?"
- When you finish the first read-through, go back and reread parts that interest you, parts that bother you (and work on them), and parts you feel especially proud of

---

## Days 17-20: Clean Up

1. Transcribe notes, set timer, say focus statements

### Daily tasks for each of these 4 days:
- Continue to make revisions on the pink copy of your manuscript. Is there anything you can say more clearly?
- Go back and re-read your Purpose Statement. Does your work fulfill the purpose?
- Does your manuscript answer the 8 to 12 reasons you thought of for why the reader should read your book?
- Insert the introduction you wrote on Day 1 just after the Table of Contents and rewrite if necessary
- Insert your conclusion (the summary from Day 15) and rewrite if necessary
- Run your spell-check

---

## Day 21: The Last Day

1. Transcribe notes, set timer, say focus statements

### Tasks:
- Input all the changes and corrections you marked on the pink copy into the computer
- Print your finalized manuscript on yellow paper
- Read the yellow version. Is it clear?
- Make corrections on the paper as you go, then input all changes/revisions/corrections from the yellow version into your computer
- Run spell-check again
- Finalize

---

## Post-Day 21: Release and Final Revisions

### The Release Period (minimum 3 days after Day 21):
- Leave your spiral notebook at home
- Don't look at your manuscript at all. Put it away, out of sight.
- Have a good time. Do restful, fun activities.
- Totally forget about your book for a minimum of three days. Allow it to steep on the backburner of your creative mind.

### After the Release:
- Get your manuscript back out and give it a fresh read
- If it could benefit from further revisions, give yourself another week to repeat the Days 17-20 process. But don't do this just to procrastinate.

### Further Revisions / Finalization:
- Print manuscript on light pink paper again
- Read it as if you are not the writer. Is it clear?
- Make final revisions, spell-check, run word count
- Re-think the name of your book (temporary title to final title). The title sells the book, so try several different ones.
- Print final draft on white paper, including copyright information on the title page
- Fill out your copyright form immediately
- Decide whether to hire professional editing services

## Bonus: Idea Exponentials Method

A faster alternative/supplement approach:
1. Write down 10 ideas that could be the outline for a book
2. Tomorrow: write down 10 ideas for each one of those ideas
3. Next day: write down 10 ideas for each of the 10 above
4. Then fill in the blanks between all the ideas
5. Then you have a book

This method can lead to a book being written in five days. The last part (filling in blanks) can even be outsourced.

## Key Recurring Themes

1. **Timer-based work sessions** - every task is timed (1 min for affirmations, 10 min for brainstorming)
2. **Focus statements / affirmations** - said aloud before every work session
3. **Carry a spiral notebook everywhere** - capture ideas throughout the day
4. **Write fast, edit later** - never polish as you go
5. **Interest-driven chapter order** - always start with whatever interests you most right now
6. **Fill-in-the-blanks framework** - the outline makes writing mechanical, not creative
7. **Color-coded printed drafts** - pink paper for first draft, yellow for final, white for finished
8. **The Release** - mandatory 3+ day break before final review
9. **Purpose-driven self-checks** - constantly asking "does this fulfill the purpose?"
10. **Audience-first thinking** - everything starts with who reads this and why they need it
